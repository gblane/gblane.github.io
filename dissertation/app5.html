Generation of Sensitivity Maps

Equation [equ:sen_dmua] shows the physical meaning of 𝒮 in terms of a measured and actual local Δμa. However, Equation [equ:sen_dmua] is not how 𝒮 is computed. We instead utilize utilize expressions for ⟨L̃⟩ from Equation [equ:Lcom_semiInf] and ⟨ℓ̃⟩ to generate 𝒮. 𝒮 maps can be generated for any of the methods to measure Δμa from Appendix [app:dmuaMeas]. These 𝒮 values may be used to create a 𝒮 for imaging applications (Chapter [ch:DSimage]), to generate 𝒮 maps (Chapters [ch:shortLong]&[ch:DSintro]) or for simulation of dynamic measurements as discussed in Appendix [app:simSigFromSen].

Generalized Partial Optical Path-Length

Similar ⟨L̃⟩ in Equation [equ:Lcom_semiInf] which describes the path of all photons in a semi-infinite homogeneous diffusion medium, we can also consider the ⟨ℓ̃⟩ which is concerned with the path through some localized region within the diffuse medium. To do this we must introduce the following r⃗:

  • The position of the isotropic source of \(\vec{r}_{src}\) (this is typically \(\vec{r}_{src}=\frac{1}{\mu_s'}\hat{z}\) for a pencil beam at the origin).

  • The position of the detector of \(\vec{r}_{det}\) (this is typically \(\vec{r}_{det}=\rho\hat{x}\) for a detector at ρ along the x-axis).

  • The position of the localized absorption perturbation of \(\vec{r}_{pert}\) with volume of \(V\).

Using these positions we find that the ⟨ℓ̃⟩ for the perturbation at \(\vec{r}_{pert}\) with volume \(V\) is: \[{\langle \widetilde{\ell} \rangle}=\frac{\widetilde{\Phi}\left[\vec{r}_{src},\vec{r}_{pert}\right]\widetilde{R}\left[\vec{r}_{pert},\vec{r}_{det}\right]}{\widetilde{R}\left[\vec{r}_{src},\vec{r}_{det}\right]} V\] where \(\widetilde{\Phi}\left[\vec{r}_{src},\vec{r}_{pert}\right]\) is the Φ̃ at \(\vec{r}_{pert}\) given a isotropic source at \(\vec{r}_{src}\), \(\widetilde{R}\left[\vec{r}_{pert},\vec{r}_{det}\right]\) is the R̃ detected at \(\vec{r}_{det}\) given a isotropic source at \(\vec{r}_{pert}\), and \(\widetilde{R}\left[\vec{r}_{src},\vec{r}_{det}\right]\) is the R̃ detected at \(\vec{r}_{det}\) given a isotropic source at \(\vec{r}_{src}\). The expressions for R̃ and Φ̃ can be found in Appendix [app:forMod] as Equations [equ:RCom_semiInf]&[equ:PHIcom_semiInf]. This equation of ⟨ℓ⟩ is implemented in Listing [lst:lpath].

As with other complex variables, the CW version of ⟨ℓ⟩, named \({\langle \ell \rangle}_{\mathrm{CW}}\), can be found by setting ω to zero in the expressions for R̃ and Φ̃ to get R and Φ instead. Just like ⟨L⟩ in Appendix [app:dmuaMeas], ⟨ℓ⟩ has a physical meaning (while ⟨ℓ̃⟩’s meaning is less clear thus leading to the name general path-length). The physical interpretation for ⟨ℓ⟩ is the average path-length that a photon from \(\vec{r}_{src}\) and detected at \(\vec{r}_{det}\) spends inside a region centered at \(\vec{r}_{pert}\) with volume \(V\).

As with ⟨L⟩ in Appendix [app:dmuaMeas], the ⟨ℓ⟩ for FD data-types I (id est amplitude) and φ can be defined: \[{\langle \ell \rangle}_{I}=\Re\left[{\langle \widetilde{\ell} \rangle}\right]\] \[{\langle \ell \rangle}_{\phi}=\Im\left[{\langle \widetilde{\ell} \rangle}\right]\] The following sections utilize these ⟨ℓ⟩s combined with ⟨L⟩ to express 𝒮 for various data-types. It is important to note that ⟨ℓ⟩ controls the perturbation in question for 𝒮 both in terms of its location (\(\vec{r}_{pert}\)) and volume (\(V\)). Typically, ⟨ℓ⟩ is found for very small voxels, 1 × 1 × 1 mm or smaller, and then summed to find ⟨ℓ⟩ for a larger perturbation of some specific shape.

Sensitivity to Local Absorption Perturbation

First lets consider the SD set with ⟨L⟩ and its 𝒮 to a perturbation with ⟨ℓ⟩. The expression for SD 𝒮 can be written as follows for 𝒴 which can be either I or φ: \[\mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{SD}\mathcal{Y}}=\frac{{\langle \ell \rangle}_{\mathcal{Y}}}{{\langle L \rangle}_{\mathcal{Y}}} \label{equ:senSD}\] where, ⟨ℓ⟩ is written above and ⟨L⟩ can be found in Appendix [app:dmuaMeas].

Next lets consider a SS set made of \(N_{\mathrm{SD}}\) SD sets with a single source at r⃗ of \(\vec{r}_{src}\) and \(N_{\mathrm{SD}}\) detectors indexed by \(i\) at r⃗s of \(\vec{r}_{src,det}\). Remembering the notation of DSF from Appendix [app:dmuaMeas], we can express the SS 𝒮 to a perturbation with ⟨ℓ⟩: \[\mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{SS}\mathcal{Y}}= \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\mathrm{SD}}}{\left(\rho_i-\bar{\rho}\right){\langle \ell \rangle}_{\mathcal{Y}}\left[\vec{r}_{src},\vec{r}_{det,i}\right]}} {\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\mathrm{SD}}}{\left(\rho_i-\bar{\rho}\right){\langle L \rangle}_{\mathcal{Y}}\left[\vec{r}_{src},\vec{r}_{det,i}\right]}} \label{equ:senSS}\] noting that we write the ⟨ℓ⟩ a and ⟨L⟩ as functions of \(\vec{r}_{src}\) and \(\vec{r}_{det,i}\) since they are important for the sum. They are of course functions of other variables which are omitted for brevity. Additionally, it should be noted that for simplicity the expression is written assuming one source and multiple detectors, however, in general the sum is over all the SDs in the SS set.

Finally, lets consider a DS set made of two SS sets named \(\text{SS}_1\) and \(\text{SS}_2\). Using the numerator and denominator of Equation [equ:senSS] surrounded by curly brackets to represent the encompassed expression for a given SS, we can write the 𝒮 to a perturbation with ⟨ℓ⟩ for DS as: \[\begin{split} \mathcal{S}_{\text{DS}\mathcal{Y}}=\\ \Bigg(\left\{\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\text{SD}}}{\left(\rho_i-\bar{\rho}\right)\langle \ell \rangle_{\mathcal{Y}}\left[\vec{r}_{src},\vec{r}_{det,i}\right]}\right\}_{\text{SS}_1}\\ +\left\{\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\text{SD}}}{\left(\rho_i-\bar{\rho}\right)\langle \ell \rangle_{\mathcal{Y}}\left[\vec{r}_{src},\vec{r}_{det,i}\right]}\right\}_{\text{SS}_2}\Bigg)\\ \div \Bigg(\left\{\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\text{SD}}}{\left(\rho_i-\bar{\rho}\right)\langle L \rangle_{\mathcal{Y}}\left[\vec{r}_{src},\vec{r}_{det,i}\right]}\right\}_{\text{SS}_1}\\ +\left\{\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\text{SD}}}{\left(\rho_i-\bar{\rho}\right)\langle L \rangle_{\mathcal{Y}}\left[\vec{r}_{src},\vec{r}_{det,i}\right]}\right\}_{\text{SS}_2}\Bigg) \end{split}\] where we note that for a symmetric DS set, the DS 𝒮 is simply the average of the two SS 𝒮s within the set. This is because the denominator of Equation [equ:RCom_semiInf] and the DSFs are the same for the two SSs in a symmetric DS set.

A key property of 𝒮 regardless of data-type is that the sum over perturbations everywhere in the medium is one. This is the same as saying that if the Δμa occurs everywhere the apparent measured Δμa and actual Δμa will be the same. Caution should be taken however, since if overlapped perturbations are simulated when calculating 𝒮 then one should no longer expect the sum of all 𝒮 to be one.

Computational Methods to Generate Sensitivity Maps

Listing [lst:senMaps] shows the MATLAB code for generation of 𝒮 maps. This code is supported by the functions for R̃, Φ̃, ⟨L̃⟩, and ⟨ℓ̃⟩ in Listings [lst:RCom],[lst:PHIcom],[lst:Lpath],&[lst:lpath]. The key to efficient generation of 𝒮 maps in Listing [lst:senMaps] is the calculation of ⟨ℓ̃⟩ and other values for every voxel in the medium simultaneously. This is done by carefully writing the supporting functions so that they could process a vector of voxel positions instead of one at a time. Lines 179-180 show a simple way to get a list of all voxel positions within the medium, and lines 190-191 show an example of where ⟨ℓ⟩ is computed for all positions simultaneously for particular source-detector pair.

After the code generates all of the 𝒮 values for each voxel, where the voxels are considered to be small, 𝒮 is found for larger perturbations by sums. For example to get layered 𝒮 the fine voxelized 𝒮 is summed in all \(x\) and \(y\) dimensions. Also to generate maps for larger perturbations convolution with the perturbation shape is preformed (for example lines 351-354 in Listing  [lst:senMaps]), however it should be noted that the output shows the results with overlap. Therefore, if overlap is simulated the sum of all 𝒮 in the medium is no longer one.

Extension of Sensitivity to Signal-to-Noise

Using the 𝒮, we can calculate a SNR associated with each perturbation within the medium. To this aim, we define the noise equivalent absorption change as the change in absorption that results from a change in the signal at the level of the noise in that signal. We start by writing the noise equivalent absorption change (\(\sigma_{\mu_a}\)) for the three methods (SD, SS, ) and 𝒴 (either I or φ): \[\sigma_{\mu_a,\mathrm{SD}\mathcal{Y}}=\frac{\sigma_{\mathcal{Y}}}{{\langle L \rangle}_{\mathcal{Y}}}\] \[\sigma_{\mu_a,\mathrm{SS}\mathcal{Y}}= \frac{\sigma_{\mathcal{Y}}\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\mathrm{SD}}}\left(\rho_i-\bar{\rho}\right)^2}} {\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\mathrm{SD}}}{(\rho_i-\bar{\rho}){\langle L \rangle}_{\mathcal{Y}}\left[\vec{r}_{src},\vec{r}_{det,i}\right]}}\] \[\sigma_{\mu_a,\mathrm{DS}\mathcal{Y}}= \frac{\sqrt{\sigma_{\mu_a,\mathrm{SS}\mathcal{Y}1}^2+\sigma_{\mu_a,\mathrm{SS}\mathcal{Y}2}^2}}{2}\] where, in this case we have only derived the expression for DS for a symmetric DS set. \(\sigma_{\mathcal{Y}}\) is the noise of 𝒴. For I this is in units of a fraction compared to baseline and for φ the units are simply rad. Now that we have \(\sigma_{\mu_a}\) for the various data-types we can write the SNR both in terms of the apparent measured Δμa and the actual Δμa of the perturbation in question: \[\mathrm{SNR}= \frac{\Delta\mu_a_{,apparent}}{\sigma_{\mu_a}}= \frac{\mathcal{S}\Delta\mu_a_{,actual}}{\sigma_{\mu_a}}\] note that this SNR is dependent not only on the analysis method, data type (with associated noise), and probe geometry, but also on the assumed true absorption perturbation and its size.
