Near-InfraRed Spectroscopy (NIRS); and Introduction (Intro).

The purpose of this dissertation is two-fold. First, to summarize the work that I have done during my time as a Ph.D. Candidate in the DOIT lab at Tufts University. Second, to provide a guide for future DOIT lab members to understand the methods that we have utilized during my time in the lab. The dissertation focuses on the first purpose and achieves the second by expanding on some introductions to techniques beyond what exists in published work.

During my approximately 4.5 in the DOIT lab I have been able to publish 9 first author articles and present 6 times at conferences as first author. This dissertation is organized to include all of this work, but due to the amount of content in published articles alone, I have focused on those. Therefore, have opted not to include work that has not made it to publication yet, has only been presented at conferences, or has been the result of collaborations.

Table [tab:organization] provides a guide to the dissertation, and shows which of my first-author articles apply to which chapters. Overall the work is organized into three parts and an appendix. Part [prt:deepMeas] is the most important, describing our work to achieve measurements that are preferentially sensitive to deep regions within a diffuse medium; this is centered around DS. Part [prt:cereHemo] describes how these measurements have been applied to brain hemodynamics, and our phasor based analysis of those hemodynamics. Part [prt:DSspect] describes how we have applied the calibration-free advantages of DS to absolute measurement over a broadband spectrum. Finally, Appendices [app:forMod]-[app:detCoh] present all of the supporting methods for this work.

Table [tab:organization] also guides which chapters would be more suited for people with different backgrounds. I have split the backgrounds into three categories:

  • Novice: One who is in a completely different field or an underclassmen undergraduate student.

  • Competent: One who is in a closely related field or an upperclassmen undergraduate student or an underclassmen graduate student.

  • Expert: One who is in the same field or an upperclassmen graduate student or one who has completed their Ph.D. in a closely related field.

These are of course suggestions and the reader may choose to read any section in any order they wish. Links between, and references to, sections are provided whenever possible to explain where one should go for more information on one topic or another.
