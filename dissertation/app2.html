Measurement of Absolute Optical Properties

Iterative Method

The iterative method of absolute optical property recovery seeks to linearize Equation [equ:RCom_semiInf] directly without making further assumptions. For this method to work the spatial dependence of R̃ or R versus ρ must be measured. Multi-Distance (MD), Self-Calibrating (SC), and Dual-Slope (DS) all achieve this. A typical FD instrument will output I (id est amplitude) and φ from which R̃ can be expressed as follows: \[\acrshort{RCom}=\acrshort{I} e^{i\acrshort{phi}}\] In the following, we first describe the analysis of R̃ across multiple ρs to retrieve μ̃eff and then μa and μ′s. When working with R data the same expressions can be used and the property retrieved will be μeff which can converted to μa assuming μ′s is known (Equation [equ:muaFROMmueffmusp]).

To yield a linear relationship Equation [equ:RCom_semiInf] can be rewritten as: \[\ln\left[ \frac{4\pi\widetilde{R}} {\widetilde{C}_1+\widetilde{C}_1 e^{\widetilde{\mu}_\mathrm{eff} \left( \|\vec{r}_1\| - \|\vec{r}_2\| \right)}} \right]= -\widetilde{\mu}_\mathrm{eff}\|\vec{r}_1\| \label{equ:compLinR}\] where the meanings of the variables is described in Appendix [app:forMod] for Equation [equ:RCom_semiInf]. For simplicty the left-hand-side of this expression is renamed \(\widetilde{y}\) and noting these variables’ dependencies on μa, μ′s, and ρ we can write: \[\widetilde{y}\left(\acrshort{mua}, \acrshort{musp}, \acrshort{rho}\right)=-\acrshort{mueffCom}\left(\acrshort{mua}, \acrshort{musp}\right)\|\acrshort{r}_1\left(\acrshort{musp}, \acrshort{rho}\right)\|\] This expression has optical properties on both the left- and right-hand-sides, therefore it can not simply be solved. Therefore, a iterative solution is used with iteration number \(k\): \[\begin{split} \widetilde{y}\left(\acrshort{mua}_{,k-1}, \acrshort{musp}_{,k-1}, \acrshort{rho}\right)=\\ -\acrshort{mueffCom}\left(\acrshort{mua}_{,k}, \acrshort{musp}_{,k}\right)\|\acrshort{r}_1\left(\acrshort{musp}_{,k-1}, \acrshort{rho}\right)\|+\widetilde{b}_{k} \end{split}\] where a complex linear fit is repeated over multiple iterations to find the slope of \(\widetilde{y}\) versus \(\|\acrshort{r}_1\|\). The variable \(\widetilde{b}_{k}\) has been added to not force the linear fit to pass through the origin and absorb instrumental factors. An initial guess for μa and μ′s must be used to start the iteration, for this guess the linear slopes method is used to estimate the optical properties. After just a few iterations μ̃eff typically stops changing significantly and the fit is said to have converged.

The μ̃eff may be converted to the optical properties μa and μ′s. This also must be done each iteration so that the values may be used as the \(k-1\) value for the next iteration. To do this, first μ′t is found: \[\acrshort{mutp}=\frac{-2 \acrshort{c} \Re\left[ \acrshort{mueffCom} \right] \Im\left[ \acrshort{mueffCom} \right]}{3\acrshort{n}\acrshort{omega}}\] using the known and assumed ω, c, and n. Then μa is found: \[\mu_a=\frac{\Re\left[ \widetilde{\mu}_\mathrm{eff} \right]^2-\Im\left[ \widetilde{\mu}_\mathrm{eff} \right]^2}{3\mu_t'}\] and finally μ′s: \[\acrshort{musp}=\acrshort{mutp}-\acrshort{mua}\]

This method was implemented in MATLAB for SC/DS data in Listing [lst:iterMethDSR] for FD and Listing [lst:iterMethDSI] for CW data assuming μ′s is known and relying on Equation [equ:muaFROMmueffmusp] as in Part [prt:DSspect], , , and .

Linear Slopes Method

The linear slope method for absolute optical property recovery utilizes the zero-boundary version of the diffuse reflection from Appendix [app:forMod]. Additionally, assumptions are made to assume ln(ρ²I) is linear with ρ. These assumptions are summed up by the following requirement expressed as an inequality : \[\acrshort{rho}\sqrt{3\acrshort{mua}\acrshort{musp}}>>1\] Since this condition is not defined strict enough, it is unclear in which situations it is met (mainly because of the use of \(>>\) which is not a specific requirement). Therefore, this linear slope method is not preferred. However, it is still used due to its simplicity and as an initial guess in the iterative recovery method in the previous section. In-fact this method is implemented in MATLAB in lines 103-129 of Listing [lst:iterMethDSR] to provide the initial guess for the iterative method.

Considering that FD NIRS measures I (id est the amplitude) and φ and that both ln(ρ²I) and φ is considered to be linear with ρ, the slopes of these data can be fit. These slopes are expressed as \(Sl_{\acrshort{lnr2I}}\) and \(Sl_{\acrshort{phi}}\) for ln(ρ²I) versus ρ and φ versus ρ, respectively. These are the slopes from SC/DS or calibrated SS/MD measurements. Using these slopes, ω, c, and n the optical properties μa and μ′s are simply expressed as : \[\acrshort{mua}=\frac{\acrshort{n}\acrshort{omega}}{2\acrshort{c}}\left(\frac{Sl_{\acrshort{phi}}}{Sl_{\acrshort{lnr2I}}}-\frac{Sl_{\acrshort{lnr2I}}}{Sl_{\acrshort{phi}}}\right)\] \[\acrshort{musp}=\frac{Sl_{\acrshort{lnr2I}}^2-Sl_{\acrshort{phi}}^2}{3\acrshort{mua}}-\acrshort{mua}\]

In the case that μ′s is assumed or known and only ln(ρ²I) is measured in CW, this method may also be used to find μa : \[\acrshort{mua}=\frac{Sl_{\acrshort{lnr2I}}^2}{3\acrshort{musp}}\] In-fact this is also seen in MATLAB code in lines 80-92 of Listing [lst:iterMethDSI] where it is used as the initial guess for the iterative recovery of μa from CW data assuming μ′s.
