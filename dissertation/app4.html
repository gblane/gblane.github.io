Recovery of Chromophore Concentrations

Recovery of Chromophore Concentrations

Methods in Appendix [app:absOptPropMeas] retrieve the absolute μa and methods in Appendix [app:dmuaMeas] retrieve an Δμa. Typically we wish to convert these measurement to either absolute or dynamic changes (for μa or Δμa) in chromophore concentrations. This is done by inverting Equation [equ:muaForward] or Equation [equ:dmuaForward]. This relies on \(N_{lam}\) measurements of μa from different λs. Considering that we wish to retrieve \(N_{chrom}\) chromophores, we must measure enough λs to satisfy: \[N_{lam}\geq N_{chrom}\] For example if O and D wish to be measured, we need at least 2 measures of μa at two different λs.

"

In practice the inversion of Equation [equ:muaForward] or Equation [equ:dmuaForward] can be done in various ways. In the case of \(N_{lam}=N_{chrom}\) there is a single solution, while in the case of \(N_{lam}>N_{chrom}\) the inversion is in-fact a linear fit. Regardless of the case, we utilize the "mldivide" MATLAB function to achieve the inversion (solution or fit). Naming the MATLAB E "E", vector of chromophore concentrations (\(\harp{C}\)) "C", and μ⃗a "mua" we can write the inversion in MATLAB code:

C=E\mua;

Or in the case of Δμ⃗a as "dmua" and vector of chromophore concentration changes (\(\Delta\harp{C}\)) as "dC" we write:

dC=E\dmua;

MATLAB’s "mldivide" divide function utilizes various numerical solvers depending on if the inverted matrix (E or "E" in this case) is square, non-square, et cetera . For our case we can consider two cases:

  • If \(N_{lam}>N_{chrom}\) MATLAB will utilize "qr" decomposition to invert \(\acrshort{EXT}\).

  • If \(N_{lam}=N_{chrom}\) MATLAB will use some numerical method to find \(\acrshort{EXT}^{-1}\).

For "qr" decomposition we write E as: \[\acrshort{EXT}=\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{R}= \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{Q}_1 & \mathbf{Q}_2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{R}_1 \\ \mathbf{0} \end{bmatrix}\] where \(\mathbf{Q}_1\) and \(\mathbf{Q}_2\) have orthogonal columns and \(\mathbf{R}_1\) is upper right triangular. Now give the two cases (\(N_{lam}>N_{chrom}\) or \(N_{lam}=N_{chrom}\)) and using "qr" in the first case we can write the piece-wise function for finding \(\harp{C}\) from μ⃗a: \[\begin{split} \harp{C}=\\ \begin{cases} \mathbf{R}_1^{-1}\left(\mathbf{Q}_1^T \acrshort{muaVec}\right) & N_{lam}>N_{chrom} \\ \acrshort{EXT}^{-1}\acrshort{muaVec} & N_{lam}=N_{chrom} \end{cases} \end{split}\] or finding \(\Delta\harp{C}\) from Δμ⃗a: \[\begin{split} \Delta\harp{C}=\\ \begin{cases} \mathbf{R}_1^{-1}\left(\mathbf{Q}_1^T \acrshort{dmuaVec}\right) & N_{lam}>N_{chrom} \\ \acrshort{EXT}^{-1}\acrshort{dmuaVec} & N_{lam}=N_{chrom} \end{cases} \end{split}\]

As an example of when \(N_{lam}=N_{chrom}\), lets now consider finding ΔO and ΔD from Δμa at λs of 690 nm, & 830 nm. For this we will use the following E of ε: \[\acrshort{EXT}= \begin{bmatrix} \acrshort{ext}_{\acrshort{O}}(\SI{690}{\nano\meter}) & \acrshort{ext}_{\acrshort{D}}(\SI{690}{\nano\meter}) \\ \acrshort{ext}_{\acrshort{O}}(\SI{830}{\nano\meter}) & \acrshort{ext}_{\acrshort{D}}(\SI{830}{\nano\meter}) \end{bmatrix}\] which we will write as "E_OD" in MATLAB using "ext" followed by the chromophore name and λ to represent the various ε. Considering that we have measured Δμa at 690 nm, & 830 nm written as "dmua690" and "dmua830", we can find ΔO and ΔD ("dO" and "dD") using the following code:

E_OD=[extO690, extD690;...
  extO830, extD830];
dC_OD=E_OD\[dmua690; dmua830];
dO=dC_OD(1);
dO=dC_OD(2);

Notice that even though there is a solution to the inversion, we still use "mldivide" since it is more general and concise in code.

Finally as an example of \(N_{lam}>N_{chrom}\), we may consider finding O ("O"), D ("D"), W ("W"), and L ("L") using 1000 λs. In this case lets say we measured μ⃗a called "mua" such that it has "size(mua)" equal to "[1000,1]", and we have ε vectors (\(\harp{\varepsilon}\)) for O ("extO"), D ("extD"), W ("extW"), and L ("extL") which also all have "size()" equal to "[1000,1]".

E_ODWL=[extO, extD, extW, extL];
C_ODWL=E_ODWL\mua;
O=C_ODWL(1);
D=C_ODWL(2);
W=C_ODWL(3);
L=C_ODWL(4);

Therefore, regardless of the number and type of chromophores, dynamic or absolute, and number of wavelengths the "mldivide" function may be used to invert Equation [equ:muaForward] or Equation [equ:dmuaForward].
