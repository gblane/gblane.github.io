Determination of Coherence

Determination of Coherence

We identify times and frequencies of significant coherence between two signals in a wavelet scalogram by comparing the measured coherence to a threshold generated with random surrogate data (Chapter [ch:signCoh]). This is especially important in CHS analysis which focuses on signals pairs involving ΔO, ΔD, and ΔA. However, in general, these methods can be applied to any pair of signals for which you wish to find times and frequencies where they are significantly coherent.

Coherence

𝔠 is a metric used to examine the relationship between two signals. Take the two signals \(x(t)\) and \(y(t)\) which are a function of time (\(t\)). Given these two signals we can discuss the 𝔠 between them, written as \(\mathfrak{C}(\widetilde{x}(f), \widetilde{y}(f))\), where \(\widetilde{x}(f)\) and \(\widetilde{y}(f)\) are the phasors of \(x(t)\) and \(y(t)\), respectively at frequency \(f\).

Depending on the methods utilized to find the signal’s phasors, 𝔠 may be a function of frequency (\(f\), corresponding to the phasor frequency) or both \(f\) and \(t\) (if the analysis allows for time resolved phasors). Values of coherence are bounded by 0, & 1 (\(\acrshort{coh}\in[0,1]\)) taking the value of 0 if \(x\) and \(y\) have no linear relationship whatsoever and 1 if they are related by a perfect linear system. For example take the time domain transfer function (id est impulse response) \(h(t)\), then we can write the following: \[\begin{split} \mathfrak{C}(\widetilde{x}(f), \widetilde{y}(f))\\ \begin{cases} = 1 & y(t)=h(t) \otimes x(t) \\ \in [0, 1) & otherwise \end{cases} \end{split}\] which is equivalent to: \[\begin{split} \acrshort{coh}(\widetilde{x}(f), \widetilde{y}(f))\\ \begin{cases} = 1 & \widetilde{y}(f)=\widetilde{h}(f) \times \widetilde{x}(f) \\ \in [0, 1) & otherwise \end{cases} \end{split}\] However, in reality two signals are not related with a perfect linear system, therefore realistically \(\acrshort{coh}\in[0,1)\) for real world measurements.

As mentioned above if the phasors are time resolved such as in a spectrogram or wavelet scaleogram, the 𝔠 is also time resolved (as well as frequency resolved). Additionally, depending on the specific analysis methods are parameters the actual meaning of the value of 𝔠 may be difficult to interpret.

Wavelet Coherence

"

We utilize a modified version of the MATLAB "wcoherence" function to determine the 𝔠 between two signals (say \(x(t)\) and \(y(t)\)). Wavelet analysis enables the time and frequency resolved phasors of the signals (\(\widetilde{x}(t,f)\) and \(\widetilde{y}(t,f)\)). Thus, in this section (unlike previous) we show when values are functions of both time (\(t\)) and (\(f\)) to highlight this feature of wavelet analysis.

First we obtain the \(\widetilde{x}(t,f)\) and \(\widetilde{y}(t,f)\) phasors from the CWT as shown in Appendix [app:phasors]. Using these phasors we can write the wavelet 𝔠 : \[\begin{split} \acrshort{coh}(\widetilde{x}(t,f), \widetilde{y}(t,f))=\\ \frac{|\mathfrak{S}\left[\widetilde{x}^{\ast}(t,f)\widetilde{y}(t,f)\right]|^2} {\mathfrak{S}\left[|\widetilde{x}(t,f)|^2\right]\mathfrak{S}\left[|\widetilde{y}(t,f)|^2\right]} \end{split} \label{equ:coh}\] where \(\mathfrak{S}\) is a time-frequency smoothing operator. In the case of our modified "wcoherence" function, \(\mathfrak{S}\) only smooths in time (to preserve frequency resolution) and the smoothing window is a Gaussian that scales with the wavelet scale (same as the MATLAB default for "wcoherence").

Examining Equation [equ:coh] we see that the numerator is the squared magnitude of the cross-spectrum, and the denominator is the squared magnitude of the two signals phasor’s multiplied together. Therefore, 𝔠 may be thought of as a normalized cross-spectral density. Some insight may now also be gained about the limiting values of 𝔠 now. If the signals are completely coherent and the 𝔠 equal to 1, then the cross-spectral density is equal to the power-spectral densities of the two signals multiplied together. This is one way of saying that all of the power from the two signals is coupled together. Conversely if 𝔠 is 0, then the cross-spectral density is also 0 which means none of the power between the signals is coupled.

Besides that idea that higher 𝔠 means a more coupled signals, the interpretation of the actual value of 𝔠 is difficult. This is in-part because the vlaue of 𝔠 is dependent on analysis parameters and importantly \(\mathfrak{S}\). As the window size of \(\mathfrak{S}\) becomes smaller 𝔠 becomes biased to larger values, and if no smoothing is applied (\(\mathfrak{S}\) having a window of one time-frequency pixel) 𝔠 will be 1. Therefore there is a trade-off between time-frequency resolution and the ease of interpretation of 𝔠. "

Coherence Thresholding

Since the interpretation of the actual value of 𝔠 is difficult, we opt to make a statement about the significance. To do this we do a test for significance at each time-frequency pixel in the wavelet scalogram. The threshold for 95 % significance is found using the method in Chapter [ch:signCoh]. Then this threshold map is compared to the 𝔠 map for the two signals of interest. Regions where the 𝔠 is above threshold are considered to be times and frequencies of significant coherence. Only these significant coherence regions are used in further analysis.

Coherent Hemodynamics Spectroscopy Thresholding

In CHS the region of significant coherence is determined for 𝔠(T̃,Ã), 𝔠(Õ,Ã), and 𝔠(D̃,Ã) since ABP is assumed to be the chief hemodynamic driver. This is with the goal of finding time-frequency regions for the analysis of T̃/Ã and D̃/Õ. For T̃/Ã only areas in time-frequency where 𝔠(T̃,Ã) is significant are used for analysis. While for D̃/Õ only times-frequency areas where 𝔠(Õ,Ã) and 𝔠(D̃,Ã) is significant is used for analysis. This selection of only coherent phasor ratio vectors is the reason for the coherent name of CHS.
